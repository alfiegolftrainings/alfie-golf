Joe, you've told me your two big frustrations are tee shots and putting — and honestly, at a 20 handicap those are the two areas where cleaning things up will drop shots fastest. Since you've got three sessions a week to work with, we're splitting this week's plan across two focused sessions: one on the range for your tee shots, one on the putting green. No prior structured practice? That's fine — we're keeping the drill count low on purpose so you can lock in on quality reps, not just hit balls.

Drills
Range Session — 45 min
1
Alignment Station with Gate
20 min
What to do

Lay one alignment rod (or a club) on the ground pointing at your target, just outside the ball line. Place a second rod parallel to it, along your toe line. Before every single shot, step behind the ball, pick a specific target in the distance — a flag, a pole, the edge of a net — then set your feet parallel to the rod along your toe line. Your feet must be aligned along that ground rod on every single rep — this is the non-negotiable.

Now add a gate: stick two extra alignment sticks in the ground about 3 feet in front of your ball, just wide enough for the ball to fly through cleanly. This gives you instant feedback on your starting direction.

Tee up a ball and hit driver. Hit 20 tee shots this way. After each shot, note whether the ball went through the gate, missed right, or missed left. Don't worry about curve yet — just starting direction.

Why this matters for you

At a 20 handicap, most tee shot problems aren't swing problems — they're setup problems. Poor alignment compounds over 4+ rounds a month, costing you fairways on every hole. This drill trains your eyes and feet so that aiming becomes automatic, not a guess. The gate adds a layer of honest feedback — you'll see exactly where the ball starts without having to guess.

2
Corridor Drill
20 min
What to do

Pick two targets on the range that are roughly 30–40 yards apart — two flags, two markers, or imagine two vertical lines. This is your "fairway." Tee up every ball. Hit 20 drivers, aiming to land the ball anywhere inside that corridor. Don't chase distance — chase the corridor. After each shot, call it "in" or "out" honestly. If you're spraying wide, choke down an inch.

One key swing thought: during the downswing, feel like your back stays facing the target as long as possible — don't spin your chest open too early. This stops you opening up before your hands have a chance to deliver the club on line, which is one of the biggest causes of wild tee shots.

Why this matters for you

You play 4+ rounds a month, Joe, so you see plenty of tee boxes. This drill directly simulates the fairway-finding decision you face 10–14 times per round. Getting your "in" count up here will translate straight to fewer second shots from the rough.

Putting Green Session — 45 min
3
Gate Putting
15 min
What to do

Find a flat area on the putting green — you don't need a hole for this drill. Pick a target line (aim at a specific point or a tee in the ground 8–10 feet away). Set up two gates using tees:

Gate 1 (putter gate): Place two tees right where your ball sits, just barely wider than your putter head, one on each side. This ensures your putter face is square through contact — if you clip a tee, your face was open or closed.

Gate 2 (ball gate): Place two more tees 6–8 inches further down your target line, just barely wider than the ball. The ball must roll cleanly through this second gate, confirming it started on your intended line.

Hit 20 putts. Focus on the putter swinging cleanly through Gate 1 and the ball rolling cleanly through Gate 2. No hole to worry about — just face and line.

Why this matters for you

You mentioned putting as a core frustration, and at your level the most common issue is inconsistent putter face at impact — the ball starts offline before it ever has a chance. This two-gate setup gives you feedback on both the cause (putter face angle) and the effect (ball starting direction) without needing any technology. Over your three weekly sessions, this is the drill that will sharpen your stroke fastest.

4
Clock Putting
25 min
What to do

Find a relatively flat hole on the putting green. Place four tees around the hole: one at 3 feet, one at 6 feet, one at 9 feet, and one at 12 feet (like compass points — north, south, east, west). Start at the 3-foot tees: putt from all four. Then move to 6 feet, then 9 feet, then 12 feet. That's one circuit. Do 3 full circuits. Track total putts holed out of 48. Focus on a consistent setup routine before each putt: read the line, set the putter face, then go. No rush.

Why this matters for you

Putting is half the game, and at a 20 handicap, the short-to-mid range putts are where most strokes leak away. Three circuits will give you genuine reps under light pressure — especially the 9- and 12-footers, which are exactly the distance where three-putts start creeping in during your rounds.
