Dave, 3-putts are scorecard killers — especially when you know you could be saving shots without changing anything about your full swing. This week we're going to sharpen the skill that causes most 3-putts — distance control — with a simple ladder drill on the putting green.

Drills
1
Putter Ladder
25 min
What to do
Find a hole on a flat section of the putting green. You're going to putt from 4 distances: 15 feet, 25 feet, 35 feet, and 45 feet. Pace these out from the hole and place a tee (or coin) at each distance to mark your spots.

Each round is two ladders — one going out, one coming back:

Ladder 1 (going out): Putt 3 balls from 15 feet, then 3 from 25 feet, then 3 from 35 feet, then 3 from 45 feet.

Ladder 2 (coming back): Now reverse it — 3 balls from 45 feet, then 3 from 35 feet, then 3 from 25 feet, then 3 from 15 feet.

A ball counts as "in zone" if it finishes within a putter-shaft length of the hole (about 3 feet) — whether it drops in or not.

That's one round — 24 balls total across both ladders. Between each putt, step behind the ball and visualise the distance. Don't just rapid-fire them. Take 2–3 seconds to feel the length of the stroke before you go.

The back ladder (45 ft down to 15 ft) is the real test — switching from a long stroke to a shorter one forces your brain to recalibrate, which is exactly what happens on the course when every putt is a different length.

Aim to get through 2 full rounds in the 25 minutes. If time is tight, one complete round is plenty.
Why this matters for you
At a 38.8 handicap, most of your 3-putts aren't about read or aim — they're about leaving the first putt 8 or 10 feet short or blowing it 6 feet past. That's a distance control problem, and it's very fixable. This ladder drill trains your hands and eyes to calibrate stroke length to distance, which is the single fastest way to turn 3-putts into 2-putts. Going out and then coming back stops you from just grooving one increasing rhythm — it forces genuine feel adjustment on every putt, just like on the course.
