Drills
1
Step-Through Towel Drill
25 min
What to do
Grab a hand towel or small golf towel and lay it flat on the ground about two inches behind the ball — toward your trail foot. The towel marks the danger zone: if your club catches it, you've hit behind the ball.

Take your 7-iron and set up normally. On each swing, make your downswing and then step through with your lead foot — after contact, let your lead foot step forward toward the target as part of your follow-through, like a pitcher throwing a ball. This step-through forces your weight to move onto your front side through impact, which pushes your low point ahead of the ball. If you hang back, you'll catch the towel.

Hit 10 balls this way. After each shot, check: did you miss the towel cleanly, or did the club brush or snag it? A clean miss means your low point was ahead of the ball — that's a pass. If you catch the towel, your low point is too far back.

After 10 with the 7-iron, move to your 5-iron and hit 10 more. Then finish with whatever your longest iron or hybrid is — 10 more. That's 30 balls total. The step-through will feel exaggerated at first — that's fine. Let the momentum carry you forward. You're training your body to commit to the front side, not just think about it.

On the mat (Session 1), you won't get divot feedback, but the towel tells you everything you need to know. On the grass (Session 2), you get both — check that your divots start at or just ahead of where the ball sat for extra confirmation.
Why this matters for you
At 10.8 with approach play as your stated frustration and -3.50 SG APP, the most likely culprit is inconsistent low point — catching it a touch heavy or thin under pressure. The towel gives you binary feedback on every rep (hit it or didn't), and the step-through physically prevents you from hanging back on your trail side, which is where fat and thin strikes come from. Once your low point is reliably ahead of the ball, your dispersion tightens and your misses become playable instead of penalty-level.

2
Distance Ladder — Irons
30 min
What to do
You're going to hit two ladders — one climbing up, one coming back down. Pick specific targets on the range for each distance (a flag, a yardage marker, a bare patch — not just "the range").

First ladder — short to long. Hit 5 balls at each target:
• 110 yards (your PW number)
• 130 yards (your 8- or 9-iron number)
• 150 yards (your 7-iron number)
• 175 yards (or your 5-iron number)

That's 20 balls. Before each swing, commit to a club, a specific target, and go through your full pre-shot routine as if you're on the course.

Second ladder — long to short. Run straight back down:
• 175 yards
• 150 yards
• 130 yards
• 110 yards

That's 40 balls total. On each shot, judge whether the ball finished within a 15-yard radius of your target (be honest — you know your distances well enough at your level to judge this).
Why this matters for you
You shoot in the 80s 95% of the time and break 70 a handful of times a year — the gap between those rounds is almost certainly approach iron accuracy. This ladder forces you to change clubs and distances constantly, just like on the course, rather than grooving one club on autopilot. Starting short and building up lets you find your rhythm before asking for full swings with longer irons. The reverse pass tests whether you can scale back down with control. It also builds the habit of committing to a specific target with every iron, which is where strokes gained on approach actually lives. No tracking tech needed — just honest self-assessment on each shot.
