Ball-first contact + course simulation

Leroy, "hit fine at the range but falls apart on the course" — that's one of the most common and most fixable patterns in golf. The range lets you hit ball after ball to the same target, same distance, same lie, with no stakes. The course forces a new decision on every single shot, often under pressure, and that switch is what causes the wheels to come off. You haven't swung this year and you're working with the driving range this week — so this plan keeps things simple and targets the two habits that transfer most directly from the range to the fairway.

Whenever the grass range is available, always practise from the grass rather than a mat. Mats mask your divots and hide whether you're actually hitting ball-first — which is exactly what Drill 1 is measuring. Mats will give you false positives. Grass tells the truth.

Drills
1
Divot Direction Drill — 7 Iron
20 min
What to do
1. Set up on the grass range. Address the ball as normal with your 7 iron.

2. Before each shot, press a tee into the ground directly at the ball position as a reference marker — not teed up, just flush with the turf. This marks exactly where the ball was sitting.

3. Hit the shot normally. After each shot, look at the ground and find your divot:
  • Divot starts before the tee = you hit the ground before the ball (fat/heavy).
  • Divot starts at or after the tee = ball-first contact. This is what you're after.
  • No divot or a scrape = thin contact. Also a low-point issue.

4. Call each shot out loud: "forward" (ball-first) or "behind" (fat/thin).

5. Hit 20 balls. If you're consistently hitting behind the tee, try feeling your weight shift slightly more onto your lead foot before the downswing — nothing dramatic, just a gentle lean.
Why this matters for you
A bogey-level player who hits it well at the range but struggles on the course often has a low-point that works on flat mats but breaks down on turf, uneven lies, and under pressure. Knowing which side of the ball your divot starts on is the most fundamental piece of ball-striking feedback in the game. On a grass range, the grass doesn't lie — every shot gives you an instant read. This drill alone, done consistently across three sessions a week, will start to make your ball-striking repeatable on any surface.

2
9-Hole Course Simulation — Driver / Long Iron / Wedge
20 min
What to do
1. You are going to play 9 imaginary holes on the range. For each hole, you will hit 3 shots in sequence, calling your target out loud before each one.

2. For each hole, follow this sequence:
  • Shot 1 — Long club (driver or 3 wood): pick a distant target representing the centre of a fairway. Say out loud: "Driver, aiming at that flag, 220 yards." Hit the shot.
  • Shot 2 — Long iron (5 or 6 iron): based on where Shot 1 landed, pick a new target representing your next position. Say out loud: "6 iron, 160 yards to that marker." Hit the shot.
  • Shot 3 — Wedge (50–70 yards): pick a close target representing your approach into the green. Say out loud: "Pitching wedge, 60 yards, that flag." Hit the shot.

3. After each of the 27 shots, mark it honestly: acceptable (would have kept you in play or on the green on a real course) or not.

4. Move efficiently between holes — this is a simulation, not a warm-up. Treat each shot as if it matters.
Why this matters for you
The gap between range and course is almost always a gap between repetitive practice and real-golf variety. On the course, every shot is a cold start: new club, new target, new distance, new decision. This drill forces exactly that at the range — three completely different shots per hole, each one called before you hit it, each one treated as if it has consequences. For someone shooting 90–95 who feels solid on the range, this is the bridge. You're not changing your swing — you're changing how you practise.
