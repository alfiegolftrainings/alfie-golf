Austin, you want to hit more fairways — that's one of the smartest things a 95-shooter can focus on, because finding the short grass off the tee makes everything else easier. This week's plan is built entirely around giving you a repeatable, confident tee shot that starts on line and stays in play.

Drills
1
Headcover Drill — Driver
15 min
What to do
Place a headcover on the ground just outside your ball's position and about one inch ahead of it — slightly toward the target. The headcover represents the right edge of the fairway (for a right-handed golfer). Your job is to hit the ball without hitting the headcover. If you clip it, that's a miss — you just found the rough.

Hit 20 drives. For each one, pick a specific target on the range that represents the centre of a fairway. A real fairway is about 40 yards wide, so picture a corridor roughly 40 yards wide around your target — that's generous, about the width of 8 cars parked side by side. If the ball lands inside that corridor AND you didn't touch the headcover, it counts.

Take your time between shots — no rapid fire. A real fairway shot on the course gets one attempt, so treat each ball here like it matters. Reset the headcover if it shifts.
Why this matters for you
At a 95 scoring level, a huge number of lost strokes come from tee shots that leak sideways into trouble. The headcover forces you to feel what a clean, on-path strike is like — any slight push or over-the-top move and you'll catch it immediately. It's instant feedback without needing a launch monitor. For a golfer whose main frustration is finding fairways, this is one of the fastest ways to tighten your dispersion.

2
Fairway Finder — Woods & Hybrid
25 min
What to do
Put the driver away. You'll hit 20 total shots split across two clubs.

Set 1 (10 balls): Grab your 3-wood or 5-wood — whichever you trust more. Tee the ball low (barely above the ground) and position it in the middle of your stance, not up by your front foot. Hit 10 shots at about 75–80% effort — smooth tempo, balanced finish. Pick a target on the range and imagine a fairway 30 yards wide centred on that target — that's tighter than a full-width fairway, about 6 car-widths across. If the ball finishes inside that corridor, it counts.

Set 2 (10 balls): Switch to a different club — your hybrid, or whichever wood you didn't use in Set 1. Same setup: ball teed low, middle of stance, 75–80% effort. Same 30-yard corridor, same target. Hit 10 shots and score them the same way.

Between shots, focus on one feel: "slow back, smooth through." If you catch yourself swinging hard, step back and restart your routine. The whole point is proving to yourself that a controlled fairway wood or hybrid finds more short grass than a full-send driver.
Why this matters for you
When your driver is misbehaving on the course, you need a reliable Plan B that keeps you in play. Most golfers shooting 95 don't practise this option — they grip the driver harder and hope. Training a controlled fairway wood and hybrid with the ball in the middle of your stance promotes a slightly more descending strike, which helps you find the centre of the face. Splitting the work across two clubs means you'll walk away knowing exactly which one to reach for on tight holes or days when the driver feels off.
