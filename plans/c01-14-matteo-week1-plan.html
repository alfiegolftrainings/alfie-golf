Breaking the shank pattern

Matteo, "shanks" — you've named it, and that's the first step. The shank feels like the most random shot in golf, but it isn't: it happens when the hosel (the socket connecting the clubface to the shaft) reaches the ball before the face does, usually because the swing path is pulling the club too far away from the body on the way down. At a 20 handicap, this is a fixable pattern — not a permanent flaw. This week we're going to rebuild clean contact from short swings first, then take what you've found into a full-swing drill with a clear indicator of what the correct path feels like.

Drills
1
Short Swing Gate Rebuild — 9 Iron
20 min
What to do
1. Using your 9 iron, forget about full swings for this drill entirely.

2. Make a half swing only — hands travel from hip height on the backswing to hip height on the follow-through (9 o'clock to 3 o'clock). Nothing longer.

3. Phase 1 — Wide Gate (10 shots): Place one ball on each side of your target ball at a relaxed width — roughly one club-head length of space either side. Hit 10 half-swing shots through this wide gate. The goal is simply to feel the clubhead passing through the centre of the lane and making face contact. The gate should feel very passable.

4. Phase 2 — Narrow Gate (10 shots): Move the two outer balls inward so the gap is just slightly wider than your clubface. Hit 10 more half-swing shots through this tighter lane. Now every pass that clips a gate ball is honest feedback — heel contact will catch the outer ball on the far side.

5. After each shot in both phases, call it: gate cleared (face contact) or gate clipped (hosel/heel contact).
Why this matters for you
Shanks carry a psychological weight that makes the full swing feel unpredictable. The short swing removes that pressure and gives you a controlled environment to locate the centre of the face again — something every 20 handicap player knows the feeling of, because you've hit plenty of good iron shots to get there. The two-phase gate progression earns confidence on the wide setting first, then immediately tests whether that feeling holds up under more precision. Two sessions a week with this drill will rebuild the pattern faster than trying to fix it in a full swing where everything happens too quickly to feel.

2
Outer Tee Anti-Shank — 9 Iron
30 min
What to do
1. Take your 9 iron and address a ball as normal.

2. Place a second tee in the ground approximately 1 inch outside the target ball — on the far side, away from your body. This creates a narrow lane: the clubhead must pass through without touching the outer tee.

3. If your swing is pulling the club too far outward (the most common cause of hosel contact), the club will clip the outer tee. If you swing through correctly, you'll miss it clean and strike the ball with the face.

4. Start at 60% swing speed for the first 10 balls — the goal is to carry the feeling from Drill 1 into a fuller swing, not to hit it hard. Build to 80% for the remaining 20 if the contact feels solid.

5. Hit 30 balls in total. After each shot, record: was the outer tee untouched?

6. If you shank one, stop. Take two slow-motion practice swings feeling the club pass inside the outer tee, then continue.
Why this matters for you
The outer tee makes the shank's cause visible in real time — no tracking system needed. For a 20 handicap, the pattern causing your shanks is almost certainly repeatable, which means the fix is also repeatable. The outer tee gives you an honest, immediate read on every single swing. The 60% speed start matters: shanks often get worse under effort because tension amplifies the faulty path. After the short swing work in Drill 1, you're carrying a feel for where the face should be — Drill 2 tests whether you can hold it in a full swing.

Closing

Matteo, these two drills work as a pair: the short swing gate rebuilds your sense of where the face is, and the outer tee confirms whether that feeling holds up when you swing with more speed. The shank is one of those problems that responds quickly to the right drill because it has a single, fixable cause.
