Moving your low point forward to eliminate fat iron strikes

Joshua, that's a really common trap — winter mats are forgiving liars. They let the club bounce through fat contact and still produce a decent-looking shot, so when you move back onto grass the truth comes out fast. This week we're resetting your low point so you start striking the ball first on real turf, every time.

Drills
1
Line-in-the-Grass Drill
25 min
What to do

Find a spot on the grass range where you can see your divots clearly. Use a tee or the edge of a scorecard to scratch a visible line in the turf perpendicular to your target line. Place the ball directly on the line. Hit shots with your 7-iron, then walk forward and check where the divot starts relative to the line. Your goal is for the divot to begin on the target side of the line — meaning the club touched the ground after the ball, not before it.

Start with 5 half-swings (hands to hip height) to get the feeling of brushing the ground forward of the line. Then build to 10 three-quarter swings. Then hit 15 full shots, checking your divot position every single time. After each shot, note whether the divot started behind the line (fat), on the line (acceptable), or in front of the line (ideal). Re-draw the line whenever it fades.

Why this matters for you

You've spent a winter on mats where there was zero feedback about where the club hit the ground — and that trained you to bottom out too early without knowing it. On grass, that's a fat shot every time. This drill gives you instant, honest feedback on every single rep. At your level (scoring 100–105), cleaning up ball-first contact with your irons is one of the fastest ways to drop shots, because fat strikes cost you both distance and direction.

2
Step and Strike
25 min
What to do

Set up to the ball with your 8-iron by placing your lead foot (left foot for a right-hander) in its normal address position first. Then bring your trail foot right next to it so your feet are together — this is your starting stance. Make your backswing with your feet together. As you start the downswing, step your lead foot out to its normal width and strike the ball. The step forces your weight to shift onto your front side before impact — exactly where it needs to be for ball-first contact.

Hit 5 slow-motion rehearsals without a ball first, just stepping and brushing the grass. Then hit 20 balls at about 70% effort, focusing on the step triggering the downswing. After those 20, hit 10 more full-effort shots. On every shot, check: did the divot start after the ball? If you're catching it fat, slow down the tempo and make the step more deliberate.

Why this matters for you

On mats, you could hang back on your trail side and still get away with it — the club would bounce into the ball. On grass, hanging back is the #1 cause of fat shots. The step drill physically prevents you from staying on your back foot because you literally have no base there until you move forward. Three sessions a week at an hour each gives you plenty of reps to groove this feeling quickly, so by next week the forward shift should start feeling automatic.
