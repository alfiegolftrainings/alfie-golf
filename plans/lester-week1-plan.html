Lester, "not being able to get chip shots to stop" at 10 yards is one of the most common short game frustrations — and it almost always comes down to where the ball is landing, not just how it's being hit. When a chip runs through the green, it's usually because the ball is touching down short of the putting surface and releasing across it, rather than landing on the green and checking with the loft of the club. This week we're going to fix the landing zone first, then build your distance control zone by zone — systematically, like a game you can actually win.

Drills
1
Landing Zone Chip — Sand Wedge
30 min
What to do
1. Set up at your chipping area with a 10-yard target (a hole on the practice green or a tee in the ground).

2. Place a tee in the putting surface approximately 2 feet past the fringe — this is your landing zone marker. Your chip must land on or past this tee, on the green itself, not on the fringe or short.

3. Use your sand wedge (or most lofted wedge available). Set up with the ball in the centre of your stance, hands very slightly ahead of the ball, weight slightly favouring your lead foot.

4. Make a smooth, unhurried swing. The goal is height — you want the ball to fly up, land softly on the putting surface, and let the loft do the stopping work. Do not try to help the ball up with your wrists.

5. Hit 25 balls. After each shot, score it using this system:
   3 points — ball lands past the tee AND finishes within 3 feet of the hole
   2 points — ball lands past the tee AND finishes within 6 feet of the hole
   1 point — ball lands past the tee AND finishes outside 6 feet
   0 points — ball does NOT land past the tee, regardless of where it finishes

6. Keep a running total as you go.
Why this matters for you
The reason 10-yard chips won't stop is almost always the same: the ball is landing short of the green and rolling across the fringe rather than landing on the putting surface and checking with loft. At a 22 handicap, this is not a swing problem — it's a landing zone problem. The points system makes that distinction visible on every single shot: a chip that misses the landing zone scores zero no matter how close it finishes, because that's exactly what it'll do on the course — run past.

/ 75 total points
2
Zone Ladder — Sand Wedge, Gap Wedge, Pitching Wedge
25 min
What to do
1. From the same 10-yard position outside the green, lay 3 clubs or alignment sticks flat on the putting surface, parallel to each other, creating 3 zones. Space them 3–6 feet apart, starting just past the fringe and working toward the hole.

2. Hit 5 balls aiming at Zone 1 (closest to you). Count how many balls actually land inside Zone 1. Then move to Zone 2 and repeat, then Zone 3. You are not trying to string consecutive shots — just hit 5 at each zone and record your accuracy.

3. As you move through the zones, change your approach to control distance:
   Zone 1 — sand wedge, ball in centre of stance
   Zone 2 — gap wedge, ball slightly further back in stance
   Zone 3 — pitching wedge, ball further back still — lower, more running flight

4. The point of changing clubs is to feel how loft and ball position work together to control landing distance. Don't try to manufacture distance by swinging harder — let the club do the work.
Why this matters for you
Once the landing zone clicks, the next piece is being able to choose where on the green the ball lands — not just "on the green" but the right part of the green. This drill builds that skill zone by zone and teaches you something you'll use for the rest of your game: a pitching wedge from 10 yards runs, a sand wedge from 10 yards stops. Knowing which club to reach for depending on how much green you have is what separates a 22 handicap from a 15.
Score to record
/ 15 balls in aimed zone
