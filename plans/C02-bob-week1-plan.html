Total time

Location
Driving range (grass bays)
This week's focus
Consistent ball-first contact

Bob, inconsistent contact is one of the most common — and most fixable — frustrations in golf. When you're not sure whether the club is going to catch it clean, fat, or thin, it's almost impossible to trust any swing you make. This week we're going to zero in on finding a consistent low point so you start striking the ball first, ground second, every time.

Because this is your first structured practice plan, I'm keeping the drill count intentionally low. Fewer drills, more reps, faster improvement.

Drills
1
Low-Point Drill
30 min
What to do

On the grass range, draw a line in the turf (or lay a thin tee flat on the ground) perpendicular to your target. Place the ball directly on the line or just in front of it (target side). Using a 7-iron, hit 40 balls with one single goal: make your divot start on the TARGET side of the line — not behind it, not on top of it.

Start with half-swings (arms to 9 o'clock). Hit 15 balls this way. Then move to three-quarter swings for 15 balls. Finally, hit 10 balls at full speed.

After each shot, walk forward and check where the divot starts relative to the line. If the divot starts behind the line, that's a fat shot — reset and go again. Don't worry about where the ball goes. The ONLY thing you're tracking is divot location.

Why this matters for you

At a handicap of 100, inconsistent contact is the single biggest barrier between you and dropping serious shots. You don't need a swing overhaul — you need the clubhead to bottom out in the right place. This drill trains your body to find that spot. Once your low point moves forward consistently, fat and thin shots shrink dramatically, and you'll feel a compression on the ball you probably haven't felt before.

Score to record
Target-side divots
/ 40
2
Gate Drill — Irons
25 min
What to do

On the grass range, place two balls on the ground to form a gate — one on either side of where your clubhead will pass through. The gap between the two gate balls should be just slightly wider than the width of your iron head. Place the ball you're hitting in the centre of the gate, in your normal ball position.

Hit 30 balls total, rotating through three clubs: 10 with your pitching wedge, 10 with your 9-iron, and 10 with your 7-iron. Your only goal is to swing the clubhead cleanly through the gate without touching either gate ball. If you strike a gate ball, that rep doesn't count as a pass.

Keep the same feeling from the low-point drill: hands slightly ahead at impact, brushing the grass on the target side of the ball. When you switch clubs, re-set the gate width — a 7-iron head is wider than a pitching wedge, so nudge the gate balls out slightly each time you move up. Reset the gate balls if they get knocked away.

Why this matters for you

Inconsistent contact doesn't just hurt you on one club — it shows up across the bag. The gate gives you instant, unmistakable feedback: you'll hear and feel it the moment you clip a gate ball. By cycling through three different irons, you're training your body to find the centre of the face regardless of club length or lie angle. That's how the consistency you're looking for actually gets built — not by grooving one club, but by teaching the same clean strike with several.
