Total time

Focus
Stopping the driver hook by controlling clubface rotation through impact

A hook off the tee with the driver is one of the most frustrating misses in golf — you see the ball start okay and then watch it dive left and disappear. At a 24 handicap, the good news is that a hook usually means you're generating decent clubhead speed; the problem is the clubface is closing too much relative to your path. This week we're going to fix that face control and get you finding fairways.

Note: I've kept the drill count intentionally focused — two drills, sixty minutes, no overload. Quality reps beat scattered effort every time.

Drills
1
Split Grip Release Control
25 min
What to do

Take your driver and split your hands apart on the grip — leave about a 2-inch gap between your trail hand and lead hand. Make 5 slow-motion swings (no ball) feeling how your hands and forearms rotate through impact. With the split grip, you'll immediately notice if your trail hand is rolling aggressively over your lead hand — that's what's slamming the face shut and causing the hook.

After the 5 rehearsals, tee up a ball and hit 5 shots with the split grip at about 60% speed. Focus on keeping the back of your lead hand pointing at the target through impact rather than flipping toward the sky. The ball will probably go shorter and maybe slightly right — that's perfect.

Now re-grip normally and hit 10 full-speed drivers, carrying the same feeling of a quieter release. Alternate: 3 split-grip swings, then 3 normal swings. Complete 3 rounds of this alternation (18 normal-grip shots total).

Why this matters for you

At 24 handicap with a hook, the most common cause is an overactive trail hand snapping the face closed. The split grip isolates exactly where this happens and gives you a physical sensation you can take to the course. You don't need a swing overhaul — you need your hands to stop fighting each other through the ball.

Straight or fade
/ 18
2
Headcover Gate — Driver
30 min
What to do

Place a headcover on the ground about one clubhead-width outside the ball, just forward of where you tee up (roughly opposite your lead foot). The headcover sits on the far side of the ball from you — it's a wall that punishes any swing path that comes too far from the inside with a closed face, which is exactly what produces your hook.

Hit 5 warm-up shots at 70% speed. If you strike the headcover, the path is too in-to-out and the face is shutting down — move the headcover half an inch further out and rebuild. Once you can clear it at 70%, move to full speed.

Hit 20 full-speed drivers with the headcover in place. Before each shot, use the same quiet-hands feel from the split grip drill. A clean miss of the headcover combined with a straight or slight fade ball flight is a success. If you clip the headcover, reset it, step back, take a practice swing, and go again — don't rush.

After every block of 5 shots, step away from the mat for 30 seconds. This keeps each swing intentional and prevents you from zoning out and swinging around the headcover rather than fixing the path.

Why this matters for you

Your hook comes from a combination of a closed face and a path that's too far from the inside — the headcover makes that mistake impossible to ignore because you'll physically hit it. At 24 handicap, you don't need complicated swing thoughts on the course; you need one reliable feeling that stops the face from slamming shut. This drill brands that feeling into your muscle memory with instant, obvious feedback on every single rep.

Clean strikes
/ 20
Closing

You're working on something very fixable — a hook is closer to a good swing than a slice is, and the adjustment is smaller than you might think. Stay patient with the split grip drill this week; it'll feel awkward at first but the feedback is immediate. Track your clean strikes past the headcover and let's see where you land — that number is going to tell us exactly what to focus on next.
