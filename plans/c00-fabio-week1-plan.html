Focus
Eliminating the driver hook by controlling face and release

That hook off the tee is costing you more than just distance — it's taking fairways out of play and putting pressure on the rest of your hole before you've even hit your second shot. This week we're going straight at the root cause: clubface closing too much relative to your path. Two focused drills, one on the range and one you can take to the course.

Drills
1
Split Grip Release Control
25 min
What to do

Take your driver and split your hands apart on the grip — leave about a 2-inch gap between your trail hand and lead hand. Make 5 slow-motion practice swings feeling how your hands and forearms rotate through impact. With the split grip, you'll immediately feel if your trail hand is rolling over your lead hand too aggressively — that's what's slamming the face shut and producing the hook.

Now hit 20 balls with the split grip at about 60% effort. Focus on keeping the clubface pointing at or just right of target through the hitting zone. Your trail palm should feel like it faces the sky a beat longer than usual through impact. After those 20, re-grip normally and hit 10 balls at 75% effort, carrying the same feeling of a quieter release. Pick a specific target on the range and note how many of the final 10 normal-grip shots start on line or fade — versus hooking left.

Why this matters for you

At a 24 handicap, the hook usually isn't coming from an advanced in-to-out path — it's almost always the hands flipping the face closed through impact. The split grip isolates exactly that feeling and gives you immediate feedback. You don't need to rebuild your swing; you need to calm down the release so the face stays square a fraction longer.

2
Corridor Drill — Driver
30 min
What to do

Place two alignment aids (tees, headcovers, or range markers) on the range to define a corridor roughly 30 yards wide, centred on your target. If your range doesn't have markers, pick two existing targets that are about 30 yards apart and use them as your left and right boundaries.

Hit 20 driver shots at 80% effort — not full send. Before each shot, make one split-grip rehearsal swing from the previous drill to reset the feel. Your only job is to land the ball inside the corridor. After every 5 balls, step behind your station and assess the pattern: are misses still left, or are they starting to spread right?

Finish with 5 "pressure" drives: tell yourself each one is the first tee shot of a round. Full commitment, same corridor target.

Why this matters for you

You said your frustration is the hook with driver from the tee — this drill puts you in a realistic scenario where you're aiming at a fairway-width target and building trust that the ball won't snap left. At 24 handicap, the biggest scoring gains come from keeping the ball in play off the tee, and this trains exactly that. Tracking the corridor hits gives you a concrete number to beat next week.

Closing

Taming that hook is one of the fastest ways to start dropping scores from 24 — keeping the ball in play off the tee takes all the stress off your approach shots. Stick with the split grip warm-up before every range session this week, even if it's just 5 swings. The quieter release will start to feel normal faster than you think.
