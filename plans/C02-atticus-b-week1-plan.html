Atticus, you told me the slice creeps in halfway through the round off the tee — that's almost always a sequencing issue where the upper body starts firing early as fatigue or tension builds. Drills
A
Headcover Drill
30 min
What to do
Place a headcover (or towel) just outside the ball, roughly 2 inches to the right and 1 inch behind it (for a right-handed golfer). If your club swings over-the-top — the move that produces your slice — you'll hit the headcover. You'll know immediately.

Before each swing, set up with the feeling of "Back to the Target" — at address, feel your shoulder blades pointing toward the target, then on the backswing, actually get your back fully facing the target. On the downswing, your ONE thought is to keep your back facing the target as long as possible before letting the club release. This delays the upper body, drops the club to the inside, and makes it nearly impossible to cut across the ball.

Hit 25–30 drivers total. Break them into blocks of 5:
• Balls 1–5: Half-speed swings. Exaggerate the "back to target" feel. Don't care about distance.
• Balls 6–10: Three-quarter speed. Same feel. Check: are you missing the headcover?
• Balls 11–20: Full speed. Commit to the feel. Use your tracking system to note start direction and shot shape.
• Balls 21–25 (or 30): Simulate on-course pressure — pick a specific fairway target, go through your full pre-shot routine, then hit. One ball at a time, no rapid fire.

If you hit the headcover, don't adjust the headcover — adjust you. Slow down, re-commit to keeping your back turned longer, and try again.
Why this matters for you
At a 17 handicap, you're not slicing because you can't hit the ball — you're slicing because something breaks down mid-round. That "something" is almost always the upper body spinning open too early in the downswing, which throws the club outside and across the ball. The headcover gives you instant, honest feedback on path, and the "back to the target" feel directly prevents the early rotation that causes your slice to show up on the back nine. Your range tracking system is perfect here — watch for the shot shape shifting from a left-to-right curve toward straight or even a slight draw as the session goes on.

B
Step & Strike
30 min
What to do
Lay a towel flat on the ground about 1 inch behind the ball, perpendicular to your target line. The towel marks the ground you should NOT be hitting — if your club catches the towel before the ball, your low point is too far back and you're hitting it fat.

Set up with a 7-iron. Start with your feet together, ball positioned in the centre of your stance. On the backswing, lift your lead foot slightly off the ground. As you start the downswing, step your lead foot forward (toward the target) and plant it firmly before the club reaches the ball. Then swing through. The step forces your weight onto your lead side before impact, which moves your low point forward — ahead of the ball, where it needs to be for clean iron contact.

Hit 25–30 balls total. Break them into blocks:
• Balls 1–5: Slow-motion step-throughs. Feel the lead foot plant before the club arrives. Don't worry about where the ball goes.
• Balls 6–10: Half-speed. Same step, start noticing contact quality — are you catching ball first, or clipping the towel?
• Balls 11–20: Three-quarter to full speed. Keep the step feel but let the swing become more natural. The step might shrink to a subtle press rather than a full lift — that's fine, as long as you feel your weight moving forward before impact.
• Balls 21–25 (or 30): Normal stance width, no step — but keep the FEELING of stepping into it. Your weight should arrive on your lead side before the club reaches the ball. Check: are you still missing the towel?

If you keep catching the towel, exaggerate the step. Make it almost a lunge toward the target. You're teaching your body where "forward" is.
Why this matters for you
The low-point issue you mentioned is the single biggest separator between 17-handicap iron play and single-figure iron play. When your low point is behind the ball, you get fat shots, thin shots, and inconsistent distances — even when the swing itself feels fine. The step-through is one of the fastest ways to retrain your body to shift forward, and the towel gives you zero-ambiguity feedback on every single rep. As your low point moves forward, you'll notice crisper contact, more consistent distances, and a ball flight that launches properly instead of ballooning or diving.
