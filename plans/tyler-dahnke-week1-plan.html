Focus
Downswing sequencing

"I started slicing, now I go back and forth between slicing and shanking low hooks" — that pattern is very specific, and it tells us exactly what's going on. When you slice, the club travels across the ball from outside-to-in with the face open. When you overcorrect into a low hook, you're closing the face aggressively to compensate — but the path is still off. Both shots come from the same root: your upper body is spinning toward the target too early in the downswing, before the club has a chance to approach from the right direction. This week we're going straight after that root cause.

Drills
1
Back to the Target — Driver
30 min
What to do
1. Set up to a normal tee shot on the grass range with your driver.

2. Pick a target in the distance and picture a 40-yard-wide fairway centred on it — roughly 20 yards left and 20 yards right of your target line. That fairway is your only goal for every ball.

3. Take your full backswing. At the top, your shoulders should be fully turned so your back is facing the target.

4. Here is the key: as you start the downswing, do NOT let your shoulders rotate toward the target yet. Keep your back pointing at the target for as long as you physically can before the rest of the swing pulls you through.

5. Think of it this way — your hips can start moving first, and your arms can start coming down, but your upper back should feel like it is still facing away from the target well into the downswing. Almost as if you are trying to hit the ball without your shoulders knowing it's happening.

6. Hit 20 balls. After each, ask yourself two questions: (a) did my back stay closed in the downswing, or did my shoulders spin early? (b) did the ball land in the fairway?

7. If the ball is still slicing hard: your shoulders are firing the moment the downswing starts — slow everything down and exaggerate the feeling of staying closed.

8. If the ball starts going left: that is a sign the path is improving. Don't chase it further — keep the grip pressure light.
Why this matters for you
The slice-to-hook cycle you described almost always comes from the upper body rotating toward the target before the club has time to come from the right direction. The slice happens when the face is open to that outside-in path; the hook happens when you overcorrect the face to fix the slice — but the path is still off. Neither fix solves the real problem. Keeping your back to the target in the downswing breaks the early-rotation habit, brings the club on a better path, and removes the reason to compensate with the face. You came back to the game with some feel from your junior years — that is genuinely useful here, because you can sense when a movement is "off." Trust that instinct.

2
Split Grip Drill (Rory McIlroy) — Driver
10 min
What to do
1. Set up to a normal tee shot on the grass range with your driver.

2. Place your lead hand (top hand) in its normal grip position. Then slide your trail hand (bottom hand) approximately 4–6 inches lower down the shaft than normal — so your two hands are clearly separated.

3. With this split grip, take the club to the top of your backswing as normal.

4. From the top, perform 2–3 pumps: bring your arms down as if beginning the downswing — stopping at around hip height — then swing back up to the top. Repeat this 2–3 times. The focus during each pump is the feeling of your arms dropping straight down in front of your trail hip, without your shoulders spinning toward the target.

5. After your final pump, slide your trail hand back up to join your lead hand in your normal grip position — do this without losing your balance or resetting your stance.

6. Make a full swing immediately, trying to replicate the same feeling of the arms dropping freely that you felt during the pumps.

7. Hit 20 balls this way. Track where each one lands.
Why this matters for you
The split grip physically makes it easier to feel your arms working independently from your body rotation. When your hands are apart on the club, your upper body cannot dominate the downswing the way it does in a normal grip — the arms have to lead. The pump rehearsals give you 2–3 repetitions of the correct feeling before each real swing, so by the time you re-grip and swing, your body already knows what "right" feels like. For someone going back and forth between slice and hook, this drill cuts through the confusion by making the correct movement obvious rather than theoretical.
