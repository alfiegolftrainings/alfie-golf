Consistent ball-first contact and centred strike across the iron set

Nate, a 4.1 who knows his irons should be sharper — that day-to-day inconsistency from 4-iron through 7-iron is the kind of thing that turns a potential 68 into a 74 without a single dramatic miss. This week's session is built around locking in strike quality across your iron range, then finishing with a wedge cooldown to keep your distance control honest.

Drills
1
Towel Drill
20 min
What to do

Place a towel flat on the ground roughly 3–4 inches behind the ball. The goal is to strike the ball cleanly without the clubhead catching the towel on the way through. Hit 15 balls with your 7-iron, then 15 balls with your 5-iron. Reset the towel position as needed to keep the distance consistent. Track each club separately — a clean strike that misses the towel counts; any contact with the towel does not.

Why this matters for you

At 4.1, your misses aren't massive — they're subtle low-point shifts that show up as thin or slightly heavy contact, and those are exactly the "4–7 ball striking inconsistencies" you're feeling day to day. The towel gives you instant, binary feedback on whether your low point is in front of the ball. Splitting the score between 7-iron and 5-iron will also show you if the inconsistency is worse with the longer club, which tells us where to dig deeper next week.

2
Gate Drill
25 min
What to do

Place two balls on the ground forming a gate just slightly wider than the clubhead, one ball on each side of your hitting ball. The gate should be tight enough that an off-centre path or a mis-hit will contact one of the gate balls. Hit 20 balls with your 6-iron, then 20 balls with your 4-iron. A successful rep means the ball is struck cleanly and neither gate ball is disturbed. Track each club separately.

Why this matters for you

This pairs perfectly with the towel drill — the towel fixes your low point, and the gate fixes your path and face alignment through impact. For a single-digit player chasing consistency, these two together cover the main reasons a strike feels different from one day to the next. The 4-iron reps will be the real test; if your gate pass rate drops significantly from the 6-iron, that's a clear signal your path is steepening or shifting with the longer club.

3
Wedge Ladder Cooldown
15 min
What to do

Using your most lofted wedge, pick three targets at approximately 50, 70, and 90 yards. Hit 5 balls to each distance in order — all 5 to 50 yards, then all 5 to 70, then all 5 to 90. A ball counts if it finishes within 5 yards of the target. Track each distance separately. Focus on smooth tempo and consistent landing spots — this is a cooldown, so resist the urge to steer or force.

Why this matters for you

After 45 minutes of focused iron work, this resets your tempo and reminds your hands that feel and distance control still matter. For a 4-handicapper, the strokes you save inside 100 yards are the ones that offset those days when the irons aren't perfect — and tracking each distance separately will highlight whether your partial swings are as reliable as they should be.
