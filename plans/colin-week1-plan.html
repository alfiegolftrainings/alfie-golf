Building a reliable driver through gate feedback and controlled tempo

Colin, "driver is unreliable" is one of the most common — and most fixable — frustrations I hear. When the big stick feels like a coin flip, it drains confidence on every tee box and puts you on the back foot before the hole even starts. This week we're keeping it simple: two focused drills, plenty of reps, and your tracking system to give us real numbers to work with.

Drills
1
Gate Drill — Path Check
40 min
What to do

Stick two alignment sticks in the ground about 3 feet in front of the ball, roughly a clubhead-and-a-half apart — wide enough that a straight or slightly curved shot passes through, narrow enough that a big miss won't. This is your gate.


Hit 40 drives. Before each shot, take a breath, check your setup (feet parallel to target line, ball opposite your lead heel, clubface square), then swing at about 70–75% effort. You're not trying to bomb it — you're trying to thread the gate.


For every shot, record whether the ball went: (1) through the gate, (2) missed left of the gate, or (3) missed right of the gate. After every 10 balls, pause and look at the pattern. If most misses are going the same direction, that's valuable information — it tells us your dominant miss, which shapes what we work on next week.


Use your tracking system to confirm what you're seeing — check the start direction and dispersion data after each set of 10. Trust the gate and the numbers over feel.

Why this matters for you

At a scoring level of 95, the driver doesn't need to be perfect — it needs a predictable start direction. The gate gives you instant, honest feedback on every single rep. Most golfers at your level have never seen their miss pattern laid out in numbers. After 40 balls you'll know exactly whether you tend to miss left, right, or both — and that's the foundation for every fix that follows.

2
Back to Target Tempo
40 min
What to do

Still with your driver. On every swing, your one thought is: keep your back facing the target as long as possible in the backswing. You should feel your shoulders complete their turn before anything moves toward the ball. It will feel exaggerated — like you're over-rotating. You're almost certainly not.


The checkpoint is simple: at the top of your backswing, your lead shoulder should be under your chin and your back should be pointing at (or close to) the target. If you can feel your back pocket facing the target at the top, you've got it.


Hit 40 drives this way. Swing at 70% effort. Your objective: land the ball inside a 40-yard-wide fairway. Pick a target line on the range, then picture 20 yards either side of it — that's your fairway. Use your tracking system to check each shot's landing position and whether it fell inside that 40-yard corridor. After every 10 balls, check your tracking numbers and note how many found the fairway.

Why this matters for you

An unreliable driver at your level almost always involves cutting the backswing short and rushing to hit. When the turn is incomplete, the downswing compensates — the club comes over the top, the face is open, and you get the big slice or pull. Feeling your back face the target forces a full turn, which gives the club room to drop on a better path. Scoring against a 40-yard fairway keeps you honest — it's generous enough that you won't need a perfect shot, but tight enough that a big miss won't sneak in. Combined with the gate feedback from Drill 1, you'll start to see straighter starts within this session.
