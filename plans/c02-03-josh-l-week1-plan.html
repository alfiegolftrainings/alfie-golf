This week's focus
Eliminating the two-way miss off the tee

Josh, a two-way miss with the driver is one of the most frustrating things in golf — you can't commit to a target because you don't know which side the ball's going. This week we're attacking it from three angles: face control, path correction, and a strategic backup plan that takes the big miss out of play entirely.

Drills
1
Driver Gate Drill
20 min
What to do

Place two alignment sticks in the ground roughly 3 feet in front of your ball, spaced about 4 feet apart (just wider than a fairway corridor at scale). Tee up and hit driver at your normal target. After each shot, note whether the ball flew through the gate, missed left, or missed right. Hit 20 balls total. Track three numbers: through the gate, misses left, misses right. The left/right split is the real data — it tells you whether your two-way miss is random or whether one side dominates under fatigue or pressure. If you see a clear pattern emerging (e.g. 5 left, 3 right), that's gold for next week.

Why this matters for you

At a 10 handicap you're good enough to find fairways — but a two-way miss means you can't aim away from trouble with any confidence. This drill gives you hard numbers on your miss pattern without needing a launch monitor. Once you know which miss shows up more often, you can start trusting one side of the hole.

2
Headcover Path Drill
20 min
What to do

Place a headcover on the ground just outside the ball (away from you) and roughly one inch behind it — so it sits slightly before and outside your ball position. Tee up your driver as normal. The headcover acts as a physical block: if your path is too far over the top (out-to-in) or you cast early, you'll clip it. Make smooth swings focused on delivering the club from the inside without striking the headcover. Hit 20 balls. If you hit the headcover, reset and make a few slow-motion rehearsals before the next ball. Track how many of the 20 you hit cleanly without disturbing the headcover.

Why this matters for you

A two-way miss almost always involves an inconsistent path — sometimes over the top producing a pull or slice, sometimes getting stuck producing a block or hook. The headcover forces a shallower, more inside delivery. For a 10 handicap with two practice sessions a week, building a reliable path feel is the fastest way to turn that two-way miss into a predictable one-way shape.

3
Fairway Finder — Woods Only
20 min
What to do

Put the driver away. Grab your 3-wood or 5-wood (whichever you're more comfortable with). Ball position in the middle of your stance — not forward like a driver. Pick a target on the range and imagine a 30-yard-wide fairway around it. Make controlled swings at 75–80% effort — smooth tempo, full finish, but not max speed. Hit 20 balls. For each one, honestly judge: would that ball be in a 30-yard fairway? Count the ones that would.

Why this matters for you

Josh, this isn't a consolation drill — it's a scoring weapon. At 10 handicap, the difference between a fairway wood in play and a driver in the trees is often 2–3 shots per round. A middle-stance, controlled-speed fairway wood is a legitimate tee strategy on tight holes. Building confidence with this shot now means you'll have a bail-out you actually trust when the driver feels off on the course.
