Drills
1
Distance Ladder — Two Clubs, Two Distances
40 min
What to do
1. On the driving range, identify two targets: one at 75 yards and one at 100 yards. Use the range distance markers or a measured reference point.

2. Choose the club you would normally use for each distance — typically your sand wedge at 75 yards and your pitching wedge at 100 yards. Stick with those two clubs throughout the drill.

3. Start at 75 yards. Hit 15 balls at your 75-yard target. After each shot, score it:
   2 points — ball lands within 5 yards of the target
   1 point — ball lands within 10 yards of the target
   0 points — ball lands outside 10 yards

4. Keep a running total as you go, then record your 75-yard score.

5. Move to 100 yards and repeat: 15 balls, same scoring system.

6. Maximum possible score across both distances is 60 points (30 balls × 2 points).
Why this matters for you
You described "anything within 100 yards takes too many shots to get on the green" — which means the approach shot, not just the chip, is setting up the damage. When a 75 or 100-yard wedge lands outside 10 yards, it creates exactly the recovery situation you described: greenside rough, bunker, then 3 shots to finish the hole. A 25-handicapper who can reliably land within 10 yards of their target at these distances turns double-bogeys into bogeys almost immediately — and that is where the biggest scoring gains are for you right now.

2
Clock Putting — Putter
15 min
What to do
1. Before starting the drill, take a couple of minutes to putt freely around the green — no target, no score, just getting a feel for the pace and the surface.

2. Find a hole on the putting green. Place 4 tees around it at exactly 3 feet — one directly in front, one behind, one to the left, one to the right (north, south, east, west).

3. Warm-up round: hit one putt from each of the four tees — 4 balls total — to get a feel for how the putts break from different angles. These do not count toward your score.

4. Now begin your scored rounds. Start at one tee and putt clockwise, going around all four positions. That is one circuit of 4 putts. Complete 5 full circuits = 20 scored putts. Count every ball that drops.

5. Keep the stroke smooth and unhurried. At 3 feet the swing is tiny — let the putter swing through the ball, not at it.
Why this matters for you
You 3-putted on 5 of 9 holes. Most of those third putts come from inside 6 feet — the first putt runs past, and the short comeback is the one that gets missed. A 25-handicapper who converts 3-foot putts consistently eliminates those third putts almost completely. This drill builds the repeatable stroke that makes short putts feel automatic rather than stressful, which is exactly what you need when you're already managing recovery shots around the green.
