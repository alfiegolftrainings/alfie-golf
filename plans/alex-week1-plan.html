This week's focus
Shrinking approach shot dispersion through consistent setup and target precision

Alex, approach shot dispersion is one of the fastest places an 18-handicap can drop strokes — and it's almost always a combination of setup inconsistency and a vague target, not a swing overhaul. This week we're going to tighten your spread with two focused drills that give you real numbers to track and a clearer picture of where your misses actually go.

Drills
1
Alignment Station + Target Precision Drill
35 min
What to do
Place one alignment rod (or club) on the ground parallel to your target line, about 6 inches outside your ball. This is your body-line reference — feet, hips, and shoulders all run along it every single shot.

Now pick a specific target at roughly your 7-iron distance — a flag, a yardage sign, a discoloured patch of grass, anything precise. Not "out there somewhere," but a single point you could describe to someone standing next to you.

Hit 15 balls with your 7-iron. Before every shot: step behind the ball, pick the target, walk in, align your feet to the rod, and commit. No rushed setups. After each shot, honestly judge: did it finish within 10 yards of your target? Track your count.

After the first 15 balls, switch to an 8-iron or 9-iron and repeat to a closer target. Same standard — within 10 yards counts as a hit. The shorter club should make it easier, so you'll see whether your dispersion problem is consistent or club-dependent.
Why this matters for you
At 18 handicap, a huge portion of approach dispersion comes from aim, not strike. Most mid-handicappers think they're aimed at the pin and are actually lined up 15–20 yards right or left. The alignment rod removes that variable so you can see your real shot pattern. The 10-yard standard gives you an honest, binary answer — close or not — instead of the vague "that was okay" feedback most range sessions produce. Over two sessions this week, you'll start to see whether your misses have a pattern (consistently left, consistently right, or truly random), which tells us exactly what to work on next.

2
Step-Through + Towel Drill
20 min
What to do
Roll up a small towel and place it on the ground about 4 inches behind where your ball would sit (away from the target). The towel is your feedback tool — if you hit it, your low point is too far back.

Take your 8-iron. Start with 10 reps without a ball. Make a smooth swing focused on one feeling: as you start the downswing, step your lead foot slightly toward the target (a small lateral shift, not a lunge) before the club reaches the bottom of its arc. Let your weight flow through — after the swing, your trail foot should be up on its toe and your chest should face the target. The goal is to brush the grass in front of where the ball would be, missing the towel completely.

After 10 reps without a ball, place a ball just in front of the towel for the final 10 reps and hit real shots. Same step-through feel — weight shifts forward, club bottoms out past the ball, towel stays untouched. If you catch the towel, reset and slow down.
Why this matters for you
Dispersion isn't just left-right — it's also distance dispersion, and inconsistent low point is the number one reason mid-handicappers hit one approach pin-high and the next one 20 yards short. Fat and thin contacts destroy your distance control even when your aim is good. The step-through move trains your body to shift forward naturally before impact, which is the simplest way to move your low point to the right place. The towel gives you instant, objective feedback — hit it or don't — so you're not guessing. This pairs with the alignment drill to shrink both dimensions of your dispersion pattern.
