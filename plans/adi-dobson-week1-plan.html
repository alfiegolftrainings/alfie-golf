Distance control + start line

Adi, you said "approach play" is the thing costing you shots — and at your level, it's the single fastest place to make the scorecard look better. This session isn't about swing changes. It's about building three reliable carry distances and a consistent start line so when you're 80 yards out, you're thinking about the target, not hoping for the best.

Drills
1
3 Stock Distances Ladder 12 min
What to do

Choose three targets you can clearly see from the range or chipping area — one short (roughly 40–50 yards), one medium (60–70 yards), one long (80–90 yards). Hit 3 balls to the short target, 3 to medium, 3 to long — then repeat that sequence once more for 18 balls total. Use the same club, the same tempo, and the same finish position for each distance. The goal isn't perfection — it's making your misses predictable. A shot that finishes 5 yards short every time is far more useful on the course than one that's random. Count a ball as successful if it lands within a green-sized zone around your target.

Why this drill for you

Distance control is the hidden half of approach play. You can aim perfectly and still miss every green if you don't know exactly how far your wedge carries at a smooth tempo. Three owned distances means three situations where you step up knowing the answer — not guessing.

2
Start-Line Gate 10 min
What to do

Push two alignment sticks into the ground 3 feet in front of your ball, one on each side, forming a gate roughly a clubhead-width wider than your ball. If you don't have alignment sticks, two tees pushed firmly into the ground work as a fallback. Pick a target between 80 and 130 yards — a flag, a post, anything with a clear line. Hit 12 shots with your most common approach club and focus on one thing only: does the ball start through the gate on your intended line? Distance is irrelevant here. Direction is everything. If you clip a stick or tee, stop completely, walk behind the ball to re-check your alignment, then go again.

Why this drill for you

Most approach misses happen before the ball has gone 20 yards — the face isn't square and the ball leaks offline immediately. The gate exposes that instantly and gives you something concrete to fix, rather than guessing from where the ball ended up 100 yards away.

3
Up-and-Down Par Challenge 8 min
What to do

Move to the chipping area. Drop 2 balls in each of 4 different spots around the green — pick spots that simulate realistic approach misses, not flat easy lies. Try to include: one tight lie just short of the green, one from light rough, one slightly uphill, one slightly downhill. For each ball: play a chip or pitch onto the green, then putt out as if it's a real hole. Your target is to get up-and-down — chip inside 6 feet and hole the putt. If you take 3 strokes from any spot, note it and move on without dwelling on it.

Why this drill for you

Even the best ball-strikers miss greens. The golfers who shoot lower scores aren't necessarily the ones who hit more greens — they're the ones who save bogey when they miss. At a 20 handicap, converting approach misses into bogeys instead of doubles is one of the fastest ways to drop shots off your card without changing your swing.
