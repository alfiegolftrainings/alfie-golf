Mike, when both ends of the bag — driver and putter — are giving you trouble, it can feel like the whole game is fighting you. The good news is that at a 27 handicap, targeted work on these two areas will drop strokes faster than almost anything else. This week we're splitting your hour across three focused drills: a gate drill for driver accuracy, a fairway-finder drill with your woods, and clock putting.

Drills
1
Gate Drill — Driver
20 min
What to do

Stick two alignment sticks in the ground about 3 feet in front of your ball, roughly a clubhead-width apart — that's your gate. Tee up every ball. Hit 30 drivers through the gate with one swing thought: complete your backswing before starting down. For each shot, record whether the ball flew through the gate, or missed left or right. After every 10 balls, pause and note your count. If you're missing the same side repeatedly, that's useful information — don't try to fix it mid-session, just notice it and keep swinging smoothly.

Why this matters for you

At 27 handicap, your driver doesn't need to be long — it needs to start on line. A gate 3 feet in front of you strips away distance anxiety and makes the drill purely about club-face control at impact. This is one of the fastest ways to clean up wild tee shots, and tracking which side you miss gives us a clear picture of your pattern for next week's plan.

2
Fairway Finder — Woods
15 min
What to do

Grab your 3-wood or 5-wood (no driver). Pick a target line in the middle of the range and imagine a fairway roughly 30 yards wide — use two markers, flags, or anything visible as your left and right boundaries. Ball position in the middle of your stance, not forward like a driver. Make smooth, controlled swings at about 75% effort — the goal is centre-face contact and a predictable ball flight, not distance. Hit 20 balls. For each one, judge whether it would have landed inside your 30-yard fairway. If you have both a 3-wood and a 5-wood, alternate between them every 5 balls to see which feels more reliable.

Why this matters for you

When the driver is misbehaving on the course, a fairway wood you can trust off the tee is a genuine stroke-saver. At your handicap, keeping the ball in play matters far more than squeezing out extra yards. Training with a slower, controlled swing and a middle ball position builds a repeatable motion you can take straight to the course when the driver isn't cooperating.

3
Clock Putting
20 min
What to do

Find a relatively flat hole on the putting green. Place 4 tees evenly around the hole at 3 feet (like compass points — north, south, east, west). Then place 4 more tees around the same hole at 6 feet, also evenly spaced. Start at 3 feet: putt one ball from each of the 4 tees — that's one round. Do 5 rounds at 3 feet (20 putts total). Then move to 6 feet and do 5 rounds (20 putts total). That's 40 putts in all. Count how many you hole at each distance. Focus on one thing: keeping the putter head low and smooth through the ball — no jabbing.

Why this matters for you

You told me putting needs a lot of work, and at your handicap, most of those lost putts are happening inside 6 feet — the range where you should be converting. This simplified clock drill builds confidence by letting you groove the same stroke from multiple angles at each distance. Tracking your scores each week will show you real progress, which matters when putting feels inconsistent day to day.
