Ross, "inconsistency, especially off the tee" is one of the most honest descriptions of a 20 handicap game — you've got shots that work, but no reliable way to repeat them. Playing 2–4 rounds a month without a structured practice framework means the range hasn't had a chance to fix what the course keeps exposing. This week's plan uses your tracking system as the feedback tool it was built for, with two focused range drills and a putting circuit to close — so you leave with actual numbers, not just impressions.

Drills
1
Setup Anchor 20 min
What to do
  1. Lay an alignment rod on the ground along your toe line — parallel to your ball-to-target line — to guide your stance every time you step in.
  2. Place a head cover on the far side of the ball (outside, away from your feet). If you clip it during the swing, your club came from outside the target line — a classic out-to-in path that produces the pull or slice most 20 handicaps live with.
  3. Before every shot, stand behind the ball and pick a small intermediate target 3 feet ahead on the target line (a divot, a tee stub, a blade of grass). Step in, align the clubface to that spot first, then build your stance to the rod — every time.
  4. Hit 20 balls (driver or 3-wood) applying this exact sequence on every single shot. Do not skip it — the routine is the drill.
  5. After each set of 5, glance at the tracker and note the lateral spread. Jot it down — and record the number of times you clip the head cover.
Why this drill for you

Tee shot inconsistency at 20 handicap almost always starts before the club moves. Different aim, different ball position, different weight shift on setup — the ball flight looks random but the root is a setup that changes shot to shot. The tracker will show you whether your 5-ball clusters tighten as the routine becomes automatic. The head cover adds a second feedback loop: if you're clipping it, your path is the issue, not just your aim.

2
Driver Corridor 40 min
What to do
  1. Pick a target on the range. Using the tracker's alignment aid (or two distant markers), define a corridor 40 yards wide — 20 yards either side of your target line.
  2. Apply the full pre-shot routine from Drill 1 on every single ball. No exceptions.
  3. Hit 30 driver balls. After each shot, check the tracker: inside or outside the corridor?
  4. After every 10 shots, pause and look at your misses. Are they mostly to one side? That's your miss pattern — note it, then make one small aim or alignment adjustment (not a swing change) for the next set.
  5. Final 10 balls: commit to the routine with zero mid-swing adjustments. Let the process work.
Why this drill for you

A 40-yard corridor off the tee is the measurable version of "finding fairways." Right now, the inconsistency you're feeling has no number attached to it — the tracker changes that. At 20 handicap, the gap between your best and worst tee shots is likely 50+ yards sideways. Today's goal isn't to fix the swing; it's to find out where your shots actually go when the process is consistent, so there's a real baseline to beat next session.

3
Clock Putting Circuit 30 min
What to do
  1. After a few minutes of free warm-up putts to get a feel for the green speed, place 4 tees around one hole at 4 feet — north, south, east, and west positions.
  2. Putt each position in order. Hole all 4 = 1 circuit completed.
  3. Complete 5 full circuits = 20 putts total.
  4. On any putt you miss, note whether the ball missed left or right of the hole (face angle miss) or whether you misjudged the speed — rolling too far past or pulling up short (speed control miss). One adjustment only per circuit.
Why this drill for you

You have a putting green available and 30 minutes — this is exactly the kind of short, repeatable drill that builds confidence without burning mental energy. Consistency off the tee and consistency on the green share the same root: a reliable process before the stroke. Finishing today's session here ties the theme together.
