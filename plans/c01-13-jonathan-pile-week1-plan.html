Focus
Shallowing the downswing path

Jonathan, "inconsistency with longer clubs" is one of the most common things I hear — but your own read on it is sharper than most: you're right that something's going wrong after the takeaway. What you're describing as "too upright through the downswing" is a steep club path coming into the ball, and it's exactly what creates that unpredictable miss with driver and fairway woods. This week we're going to give you one feeling and one drill to start shallowing that path — and we'll use your Trackman session on Saturday to put hard numbers on where you're starting from.

Drills
1
Headcover Path Gate — Driver
30 min
What to do
1. Tee up a driver as normal.

2. Place a spare headcover (or a golf bag pocket, or a second ball sleeve) on the ground about 12 inches outside the ball and slightly behind it — on your trail side, just outside the target line.

3. Your swing thought: the clubhead must pass inside and under that headcover on the downswing. If your path is steep and over-the-top, you'll clip it. If you approach from the inside, you'll miss it clean.

4. Take three slow-motion practice swings first — feel the club drop inside, not over.

5. Hit 30 drivers. After each shot, record: did the headcover stay untouched (inside path) or did the club catch it (over-the-top)?

6. On your Trackman session this Saturday: also note the Club Path reading on screen after each shot. A number that's highly negative (e.g. -8° or steeper) confirms the over-the-top move; you're working toward something closer to 0° or slightly positive.
Why this matters for you
The inconsistency you've been feeling with longer clubs comes directly from a steep angle of attack — and the headcover gives you immediate, honest feedback without needing any technology. At your current scoring level of 95–105, getting the path even slightly less steep will make a visible difference in ball flight from session one. The Trackman data on Saturday will give you a baseline number so we can track progress week by week.

2
Pause-at-the-Top — 6 Iron
20 min
What to do
1. Pick up your 6 iron (or whichever mid-iron feels most like your "normal" swing).

2. Place an alignment stick in the ground 3 feet in front of your ball, slightly to the left of your target when looking from behind the ball. This is your start-line reference: shots that begin to the right of the stick are on or inside your target; shots that begin to the left of it are pulls caused by the over-the-top path coming back in.

3. Make a full backswing — then pause for a full count of one at the top before you start the downswing. The pause should feel uncomfortable at first. That's fine.

4. After the pause, let the lower body start the movement: feel your lead hip shift toward the target before your hands do anything.

5. This sequence — pause, hip shift, then hands — is the antidote to the rushing transition that causes the steep path.

6. Hit 20 balls. Every single one gets the pause. No exceptions.

7. Count how many shots start on or to the right of the alignment stick.
Why this matters for you
You've described your takeaway as fine — and I'd agree that the problem typically isn't going back, it's how you start coming down. Rushing the transition is the #1 cause of the steep, upright downswing you've identified. With three sessions a week available, this pause drill done consistently will start to rewire that sequencing faster than almost any other single change you could make. It works with the 6 iron because the shaft length is forgiving enough to let you focus on the feeling without the driver's extra demand.
