This Week's Focus
Centring driver contact & neutralising the out-to-in path

Russ, that big fade off the driver with heel contact — those two things are connected. When the club delivers from an out-to-in path, the heel leads into the ball, and the face is open to that path. This week we're going after the root cause: getting the club swinging more from the inside so you find the centre of the face and straighten that ball flight.

Drills
1
Headcover Drill — Driver Path Fix
20 min
What to do

Tee up a ball at your normal driver height. Place a headcover on the ground just outside the ball and about 2 inches closer to you (toward your feet), positioned slightly ahead of the ball — roughly 1–2 inches nearer the target. The headcover sits in the exact zone your club will pass through if you swing over the top on an out-to-in path.

Hit 25 drivers at 70% effort. Your only job: miss the headcover. If you strike or scuff it, your club came from outside the line. A clean miss means the club is dropping more from the inside. After each shot, note two things: (1) did you hit the headcover? (2) what was the ball flight — fade, straight, or draw?

If you keep clipping the headcover, feel like your hands drop straight down from the top of the backswing before you turn through. That sensation creates the room the club needs to pass inside the cover.

Why this matters for you

At 19 handicap, your driver is one of the highest-leverage clubs to improve — you hit it 14 times a round. That heel contact you're feeling is a direct symptom of the club approaching from outside the line. The headcover is a bold, unmissable piece of feedback — your brain will quickly learn to re-route the club to avoid it. As the path shifts to the inside, the big fade will shrink on its own and contact will move toward the centre of the face.

2
Back to Target — Speed and Path Hold
10 min
What to do

Stay with the driver. Tee up a ball at normal height. On the downswing, focus on one feeling: keep your back turned toward the target for as long as you possibly can before your body unwinds and releases through the ball. Your chest should feel like it's still facing away from the target while your arms start to drop.

Hit 15 balls, building speed across the set — start at around 60% effort and work up to 90% by the final five swings. After each shot, call the ball flight: straight, left, or right. Keep a simple tally. This drill tests whether the inside path you trained in the headcover drill holds up when you add speed. The "back to target" feel prevents your shoulders from spinning open early, which is what throws the club over the top.

Why this matters for you

Your big fade shows up most when you swing hard, because speed amplifies the over-the-top move. By deliberately holding your back to the target, you give the club time to shallow and approach from the inside — even at higher speeds. Tracking the ball flight across increasing effort levels will show you exactly where your threshold is. At 3 sessions a week, you'll push that threshold further each time.
