This week's focus
Ball-first contact & low-point control with long irons

Tucker, long irons are one of the hardest things to sharpen because they punish every small contact issue that shorter irons forgive. This week we're going to zero in on striking your long irons clean and consistent — ball-first contact with a confident, committed swing. One focused drill, done well three times this week, will do more than hitting a bucket of 4-irons aimlessly.

Drills
1
Low-Point Stripe
15 min
What to do

Place an alignment rod (or draw a line with a tee) on the grass perpendicular to your target line. Set the ball just in front of the line — about 1 inch on the target side. Hit 25 balls total with your longest iron that frustrates you (likely 4i or 5i). Use the first 5 balls as a warm-up with a mid-iron to calibrate, then switch to the long iron for the remaining 20. On every swing, your goal is to brush or take a divot that starts on the target side of the line — never behind it. After each shot, check where your club contacted the ground relative to the line. Every 10 balls, pause and note how many were clean strikes with a divot ahead of the line.

Why this matters for you

At 6.4, your swing fundamentals are strong — you're not rebuilding anything. But long irons demand a low point that's consistently forward, and even a half-inch back turns a pure 4-iron into a thin skull or a fat chunk. This drill gives you instant visual feedback on exactly where you're bottoming out, so you can feel the difference between a committed strike and a bail-out. Three sessions of this week and you'll start trusting those long irons instead of steering them.

2
Pause-at-the-Top
15 min
What to do

Using the same long iron, hit 15 balls with a deliberate pause at the top of your backswing. Complete your backswing, hold for a full second, then start the downswing. The pause should feel like a genuine stop — not a slow-motion transition. Focus on staying balanced during the pause and then accelerating smoothly through the ball. After the 15 pause swings, hit 5 full-speed swings trying to keep the same feeling of calm transition and committed contact. For both sets, mark each shot as "good contact" if the strike feels solid and centred on the face.

Why this matters for you

When long irons aren't cooperating, the natural instinct is to rush from the top — snatching at the ball to generate the speed you think you need. That rush kills your sequencing and moves your low point backwards, which is exactly the problem you're working on. The pause forces your lower body to lead the downswing properly, giving you the forward shaft lean and ball-first contact that make long irons feel effortless. At your handicap, this isn't a new move — it's removing the tension that blocks the move you already have.
