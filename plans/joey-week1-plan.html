Driver dispersion + fairway finder

Joey, "penalties off the tee or having to punch out to get back in play" — that's not a power problem, it's a dispersion problem. At a 14.3 handicap you clearly have the game to score well, but when the driver goes sideways it turns a par opportunity into a scramble before you've even taken your second shot. This week is about two things: understanding where your driver is actually starting the ball, and building a reliable fairway-finder shot you can lean on when the driver isn't cooperating. Both drills give you honest data you can act on immediately.

Drills
1
Driver Gate + Fairway Finder
30 min
What to do
1. Pick a distant target on the range representing the centre of a fairway.

2. Push two alignment sticks into the ground 3 feet in front of the ball, just wider than the driver face — they form a gate that the ball must pass through at the start of its flight.

3. Set two more sticks (or use headcovers) on the ground at your target distance, marking a 40-yard-wide corridor — 20 yards either side of your target line. This is your fairway.

4. Hit 30 drivers. After each shot, record two things:
  • Gate result: did the ball start through the gate, right of the gate, or left of the gate?
  • Fairway result: did the ball land inside the 40-yard corridor?

5. Call each shot out loud as you hit it. Don't filter — honest tracking is the only way to see your real pattern.
Why this matters for you
The penalties and punch-outs you described come from a consistent miss, and most golfers don't actually know which way they're missing — or whether it's face angle (gate) or overall shape (fairway) causing the damage. At 14.3, understanding your start direction pattern gives you something concrete to fix, not just "I need to be more consistent." The gate shows you where the face is pointing; the fairway shows you the result.

2
Fairway Finder — 3W or Hybrid at 70%
20 min
What to do
1. Pick your 3-wood, 5-wood, or hybrid — whichever feels most reliable off the tee when the driver isn't on. This is your fairway finder club for this drill.

2. Set up with the ball noticeably more towards the centre of your stance than you normally would with a fairway wood or hybrid — this promotes a slightly steeper, more controlled strike and reduces the sidespin that causes big misses.

3. Swing at no more than 70% of your maximum speed. Not a slow-motion swing — a controlled, smooth swing where you feel like you have something in reserve at the top and through impact.

4. Use the same 40-yard corridor from Drill 1. Hit 15 balls.

5. After each shot, call it: "In" (would have found the fairway) or "Out." Shape and distance don't matter — only whether it stayed in play.
Why this matters for you
At 14.3, the temptation when the driver is off is to swing harder to compensate — which makes the miss worse. The ball position change and the 70% speed cap are doing two things together: keeping the strike more centred and taking the big miss out of play. This is the tee shot you reach for when the driver isn't cooperating, and building it at 70% now means it's there for you under pressure on the course.
