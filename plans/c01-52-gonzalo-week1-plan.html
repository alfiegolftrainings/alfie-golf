Drills
1
Headcover Gate Drill (Driver)
40 min
What to do
Place a headcover on the ground just outside the ball (roughly one clubhead-width away from the ball, on the far side — the side away from your feet) and slightly ahead of it (about one ball-width closer to the target than where the ball sits). The headcover acts as a gate: if your club swings over the top and cuts across the ball on an out-to-in path, you'll hit the headcover. If you swing from the inside, the club will miss the headcover cleanly.

Hit 40 drivers in sets of 5. Before each set, step behind the ball, pick a target on the range, then address the ball. After each rep, note two things: (1) did you miss the headcover? (2) did the ball start on or near your target line?

A rep counts as a "clean pass" only if you miss the headcover AND the ball starts within a reasonable window of your target. If you clip the headcover, that's instant feedback — your path came over the top.

Between each set of 5, step away, take a breath, and reset. This simulates the gap between tee shots on course rather than mindlessly firing balls.

If the first 20 reps feel easy and you're missing the headcover consistently, move it slightly closer to the ball line (half a clubhead-width away) for the final 20 to tighten the gate.
Why this matters for you
You're a 28-handicapper shooting 95–105, and you said yourself the difference between a good round and a bad one is almost entirely off the tee. Losing 10 balls in a round is probably costing you 15–20 strokes on its own. The headcover gives you immediate, physical feedback on the exact swing path that causes your slice — no launch monitor needed. Every clean pass means your club is approaching from the inside, which is the direct fix for the out-to-in path creating that banana ball. Forty reps with this kind of instant feedback is worth more than 100 balls on autopilot.

2
Back to Target Rotation Drill (Driver)
25 min
What to do
Take your driver. Pick a specific target on the range and imagine a fairway 30 yards wide centred on that target. On each swing, focus on one feel: keep your back facing the target for as long as possible during the downswing. Don't spin your chest open early — instead, feel like your hips start the downswing while your upper body stays "closed" and turned away. This creates lag, stores energy, and naturally shallows the club onto an inside path.

Hit 25 balls in sets of 5. Swing with intent — this is not a slow-motion drill. You're trying to build speed through better rotation sequence, not by swinging harder with your arms.

After each ball, judge whether it would have finished within that 30-yard-wide fairway. If your range has a tracking system, use it to check. If not, judge by eye — be honest with yourself, but don't overthink marginal ones.

Between each set of 5, step away and reset. After each set, review your tally. You may notice the ball starting further left than usual early on — that's actually a good sign. It means you're swinging more from the inside and the face is closing, which is a much better miss than the slice.
Why this matters for you
You told me you can lose 10 balls off the tee in a bad round. The headcover drill fixes the path; this drill adds speed and consistency to that new path. Early chest rotation pulls the club across the ball — that's the same out-to-in move creating your slice. Keeping your back to the target longer lets the club drop onto an inside path with more stored energy, so you hit it further AND straighter. This feel reinforces exactly what the headcover drill is training, but now without the gate — you're internalising the move. Scoring by fairway hits ties the drill directly to what matters on the course: keeping the ball in play.

3
Wedge Ladder Drill
20 min
What to do
Using the range, pick three targets at roughly 75, 100, and 125 yards. Use tees, yardage markers, or flags already on the range — whatever is available. Grab your sand wedge, pitching wedge, and whichever club you'd normally hit for 125 yards (likely your PW or 9-iron — use whatever feels right).

Hit 5 balls at the 75-yard target, then 5 at the 100-yard target, then 5 at the 125-yard target. That's one round (15 balls). Do two full rounds = 30 balls total.

A ball counts as "in zone" if it finishes within 10 yards of the target. If your range has a tracking system, use it to measure. If not, judge by eye — at these distances, 10 yards is roughly the width of a green.

Between each distance, pause and commit to the new target before swinging. The point is deliberate distance changes, not rapid fire.
Why this matters for you
You mentioned a gap between your SW and PW distances — 70 to 105 metres with nothing reliable in between. This ladder forces you to practise controlling trajectory and swing length across three distinct distances, which is exactly where that gap lives. At your handicap, getting a wedge within 10 yards of your number on the course would save you multiple shots per round — those are the approach shots that set up easier putts and fewer big numbers. Twenty minutes here also gives your driver muscles a rest after 65 minutes of full-speed swings.
