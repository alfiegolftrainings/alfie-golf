This week's focus
Consistent contact with fairway woods & hybrids off the deck

Mark, that moment you described — standing over a fairway wood or hybrid on a second shot, knowing you need something acceptable, and instead dribbling it 60 yards — that's not a swing speed problem or a club selection problem. That's a low-point and strike problem, and it's the exact thing we're going after this week. Fairway woods and hybrids off the deck are unforgiving when contact is even slightly off, and the fix is more mechanical than mental. Let's clean it up.

Drills
1
Low-Point Brush Warm-Up
5 min
What to do
Before hitting any balls, grab a mid-iron and find a spot on the grass. Draw a short line in the turf with a tee — perpendicular to your target line, roughly in the middle of your stance. You can also lay an alignment stick on the ground instead, positioned the same way (perpendicular to the target, a few inches away from your body so you don't hit it).

Set up with the line or stick slightly behind center in your stance. Now make continuous, easy hip-to-hip swings — no ball, no target, just smooth back-and-through motion. Your only goal is to have the sole of the club brush the grass on the target side of that line every time. No divot — just a light brush. If you're catching the ground behind the line, you're bottoming out too early. If you're not touching the grass at all, you're lifting out of it.

Keep the rhythm relaxed and continuous — don't stop between swings. Think of it as a pendulum. Do this for 5 minutes straight. By the end, you should feel exactly where the club naturally reaches the ground when your body stays centered and your arms stay loose. That's the low-point awareness you'll carry into the rest of this session.
Why this matters for you
Those dribbled fairway wood and hybrid shots you described are a low-point problem — the club is reaching the ground in the wrong spot. This warm-up trains your body to feel where the bottom of the arc is before you add a ball to the equation. At 11.9 your swing is plenty good enough; this is about calibrating the one thing that makes the difference between a flush strike and a 60-yard dribble. Starting every session this way builds the habit fast.
2
Tee Sweep Drill — 3-Wood
20 min
What to do
Make sure you're hitting off grass for this entire session — no mats. Mats hide fat contact and won't give you honest feedback.

Grab your 3-wood. Place a ball on the ground and set up with it in the middle of your stance — not forward, middle. Now lay a tee flat on the ground horizontally, just in front of the ball (on the target side, about an inch ahead). The tee is your feedback tool.

Your goal on every swing is to sweep the ball and the tee off the ground together in one smooth motion. Swing easy — maybe 60–70% effort — with loose, soft arms. No forcing it. You want the sole of the club to brush the ground shallow enough to collect the ball and push that tee forward. If you're digging, the club will thud into the turf behind the ball and the tee stays put. If you're picking the ball clean with no ground contact, the tee also stays put. The sweet spot is a shallow sweep that sends both the ball and the tee forward.

Hit 25 balls this way. After every 5 shots, reset and check: am I staying loose, or am I tightening up and swinging harder? The urge with a 3-wood is to muscle it. Resist that. Easy arms, smooth sweep, ball and tee gone together.

Total: 25 balls.
Why this matters for you
Those dribbled shots at 170–200 yards almost always come from the club bottoming out too early — catching the ground behind the ball or topping it with an ascending strike. At 11.9 you clearly have the swing speed to hit these clubs — the issue is where the club reaches the ground and how tense your arms get when the pressure is on. This drill retrains the low point while also teaching you that loose arms and an easy tempo launch the ball better than forcing it. When the tee disappears with the ball, the strike is right.

3
Feet-Together Sweep
25 min
What to do
Stay on grass — no mats. Using your hybrid, set up with your feet completely together and the ball positioned in the center of your narrow stance. Make a smooth three-quarter swing and hit the ball. With your feet together, you physically cannot lunge or sway — your body has to rotate in place, which naturally levels out the bottom of your swing arc. This is exactly the shallow, sweeping strike fairway woods and hybrids need off the deck.

Hit 10 balls this way with the hybrid. Then widen to your normal stance and hit 5 balls, trying to recreate that same feeling of a smooth, balanced sweep through the ball. On each normal-stance shot, ask yourself: did the club brush the ground smoothly, or did it thump?

Switch to your fairway wood. Same sequence: 10 feet-together shots (swing at 70% effort — fairway woods are longer and harder to control with a narrow base, so keep it smooth), then 5 normal-stance shots recreating the feel.

Finish with 5 normal-stance shots with whichever of the two clubs gives you the most trouble on the course. On these, pick a target in the 170–200+ yard range and commit to it.

Total: 35 balls.
Why this matters for you
Mark, when you described standing over a second shot and mishitting it, that's a pressure moment where your body tends to get overactive — lunging at the ball, swaying, or hanging back. The feet-together drill strips all of that away and forces a centered, balanced strike. Fairway woods and hybrids reward smooth rotation, not force. Once you feel how little effort it takes to launch a hybrid off the deck when your body stays centered, those par-5 second shots stop being a gamble and start being a weapon.

Closing
