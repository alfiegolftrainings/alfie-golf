Joel, you've flagged tee shots as your main frustration — and at your stage of the game, getting the ball off the tee and into play consistently is the single biggest thing that will bring your scores down. This week's plan is four focused drills. We're not trying to fix everything at once. We're building a reliable swing path off the tee, a smoother connection between your arms and body, and a simple short game foundation so you can start enjoying your rounds more.

Drills
1
Driver Gate Drill
20 min
What to do

Stick two alignment rods in the ground about 3 feet in front of your ball, spaced roughly 3 feet apart — this is your gate. Tee up your driver and hit 20 balls, aiming to send each one through the gate. Don't chase distance. Your only job is to start the ball between the two sticks.

After every 5 balls, step away completely, walk behind the ball, pick a fresh target through the gate, and set up again as if it's a brand new shot on the course.

For each ball, note whether it passed through the gate, missed left, or missed right. The left/right pattern will tell you a lot about your tendencies — we'll use that information in future weeks.

Why this matters for you

At a scoring level of 120, tee shots often spray in unpredictable directions because there's no consistent reference point for where the ball starts. The gate gives you a simple, visual pass/fail target just a few feet away. Instead of worrying about where the ball lands 200 yards out, you're focused on the first few feet of ball flight — which is where accuracy is built. This is the fastest way to start finding more fairways.

2
Rory McIlroy Split Grip
20 min
What to do

Take your 7-iron. Grip the club with your normal hand positions, but slide your bottom hand down the grip so there's a visible gap (about 2–3 inches) between your hands. This is the split grip.

Hit 15 balls with this grip at about 70% effort. The split grip forces your arms and body to work together — if they get out of sync, the shot will feel awful and you'll know immediately. Focus on making smooth, connected swings where your chest turns through the ball.

After 15 split-grip shots, move your hands back to a normal grip and hit 10 more balls, trying to keep that same feeling of connection and smoothness.

Why this matters for you

Joel, this drill is used by Rory McIlroy as a warm-up to sync his arms with his body rotation. For a golfer scoring around 120, the arms and body often work independently — the arms swing one way, the body another, and the result is inconsistent contact. The split grip makes that disconnect obvious so you can feel the difference. With your 4+ sessions a week, this connected feeling will start becoming natural quickly.

3
Fairway Finder
20 min
What to do

Put the driver away. Grab a fairway wood or hybrid — whichever you feel more comfortable with. Tee the ball low or play it from the mat with the ball positioned in the middle of your stance (not forward like a driver).

Hit 20 balls with the feeling of a slower, controlled swing — about 70–80% effort. Pick a target on the range and imagine a 30-yard-wide fairway around it. For each ball, judge honestly whether it would have landed in that 30-yard fairway.

This is about building a "get it in play" club you can trust when the driver isn't cooperating on the course.

Why this matters for you

At your scoring level, one of the quickest ways to cut strokes is having a reliable backup off the tee. A fairway wood with a controlled swing and a centred ball position is much easier to get airborne and keep straight than a full driver swing. This drill builds the feel and the confidence to pull that club on the course when you need a safe tee shot — and at 120, a ball in the fairway beats a long ball in the trees every single time.

4
Clock Putting
25 min
What to do

On the putting green, place 4 tees around a hole — one at 3 feet, one at 6 feet, one at 9 feet, and one at 12 feet (north, south, east, west). Start at the 3-foot tees. Putt one ball from each tee. That's one circuit. Complete 3 circuits at 3 feet before moving to 6 feet, then 9 feet, then 12 feet.

Track how many putts you hole out of each set of 12 (3 circuits × 4 tees). Don't worry about the longer distances yet — the goal is to feel confident from 3–6 feet first.

Why this matters for you

Tee shots are your stated frustration, but at a score of 120 there are a lot of strokes hiding on the putting green too. This drill builds a putting routine you can repeat and gives you quick, visible progress — holing short putts feels good and builds confidence that carries into the rest of your game. Since you have access to a putting green every session, this is an easy win to add at the end of every practice.
