Drills
1
Wedge Ladder
30 min
What to do
Set up at the range with an alignment rod or club on the ground for aim. You're going to hit a 5-shot ladder at these distances: 70, 100, 120, 135, and 150 yards. For each distance, grab whichever club gets you there — don't overthink it, just trust your gut and pull the one that feels right.

Work through the ladder "up and down" 3 times. Each round = 10 shots:
• Up: 70 yd → 100 yd → 120 yd → 135 yd → 150 yd
• Down: 150 yd → 135 yd → 120 yd → 100 yd → 70 yd

That's 30 balls total. For each shot, pick a specific target on the range — a flag, a yardage sign, a bare patch. Before every swing, say the yardage out loud. After the shot, judge whether it landed within 5 yards of your target (long/short counts, not just left/right). Mark it as a "hit" if it's inside that 5-yard window.
Why this matters for you
Shooting around 90 means you're likely leaving several shots per round inside 150 yards — approach shots that end up 30 feet from the pin instead of 15, or miss the green entirely. This ladder forces you to switch clubs and distances constantly, just like on the course, so you build real feel for each yardage rather than grooving one club in isolation. Going up and down through the distances trains your brain to adjust quickly — exactly what you need standing over a 120-yard shot on the course.

2
18-Hole Up-and-Down Challenge
30 min
What to do
Go to the chipping area and putting green. You're going to play 18 "holes" — each hole is one chip plus as many putts as it takes to hole out.

For each hole:
1. Drop a ball in a different spot around the chipping green — vary the lie, the distance, and the club you use. Short chips, long bump-and-runs, flop shots if you feel brave. Move around the green so no two holes are the same.
2. If there's a bunker available, play at least 1 or 2 of your 18 holes from the sand. Drop a ball in, blast out, and putt out just like the other holes.
3. Chip onto the putting green aiming at a specific hole.
4. Putt out. Track your total strokes (chip + putts) for each hole.

Par for every hole is 2, so par for the full 18 is 36. Your goal is to complete all 18 holes and record your total score against that 36 target.

Keep the pace brisk. You have about 90 seconds per hole, which is plenty. If the chipping area only has one or two holes, change your starting position each time to create variety.
Why this matters for you
Chipping is half your frustration, and this drill puts scoring pressure on every single rep. Instead of mindlessly hitting chip after chip to nowhere, you have to read a lie, pick a landing spot, execute the chip, then finish with the putter. That's exactly the sequence you face when you miss a green shooting 90 — and you're probably missing 10+ greens per round. Tracking your total score against par gives you a clear number to beat each week.
