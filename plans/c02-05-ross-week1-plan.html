This week's focus
Consistent contact that survives fatigue

Ross, you've nailed the pattern — starting strong then watching your contact fall apart on the back 9. That's almost always a tempo and physical sequencing issue: as fatigue sets in, your body shortcuts the swing and your low point drifts. This week we're going to build a swing that holds up when your legs and focus get tired.

Drills
1
Fatigue-Proof Contact
30 min
What to do
Set up a "low-point station": place a tee in the ground even with your lead heel. Your goal is to brush the ground on the TARGET side of that tee (toward where the ball would go), not behind it. Start with a 7-iron or 8-iron.

Here's the key — you're going to simulate back-9 fatigue:

• Treat every single ball like it's a shot on the course. Go through your full pre-shot routine — the same practice swings, the same setup, the same process you'd use standing over a ball on hole 14. No rapid-fire range hitting.
• Hit 5 balls this way, with your full routine before each one.
• Then do 10 deep squats, right there on the range.
• Hit 5 more balls, same club, same target, full routine every time.
• Repeat this cycle 6 times (30 balls total, 5 sets of squats between).

After each set of 5 post-squat swings, note how many times your divot or ground contact was on the target side of the tee. The squats aren't about getting a workout — they're about training your body to find the low point even when your heart rate is up and your legs are heavy. That's your back 9. And by using your real pre-shot routine every time, you're building the habit of going through your full process even when you're tired — which is exactly what falls apart late in a round.
Why this matters for you
As someone shooting around 105, the swing you have when you're fresh is clearly working — you said yourself you start strong. The issue isn't your swing, it's that your swing changes when you're tired. By deliberately practising under mild physical stress with your full on-course routine, you're teaching your body the one move that matters most: hitting the ball before the ground, even on hole 15.

2
Pause-at-the-Top Tempo Holds
25 min
What to do
Same 7-iron or 8-iron. Pick a target on the range about 120–130 yards out (or whatever your comfortable 7-iron distance is — doesn't matter exactly).

Take the club back to the top of your backswing and HOLD for a full "one-Mississippi" count. Then swing through to the target. The pause should feel uncomfortably long at first — that's the point. You're breaking the habit of rushing from the top, which is the first thing that happens when fatigue creeps in on the course.

Hit 30 balls this way. Every single one gets the full pause. No exceptions — if you catch yourself skipping it, that rep doesn't count.

For the last 10 balls, remove the pause but try to keep the same smooth, unhurried feeling through transition. Rate each swing: did it feel as smooth as the paused version? Track how many of those 10 feel controlled.
Why this matters for you
When you're fatigued on the back 9, your body wants to get the swing over with quickly. That rush kills your sequencing — arms outrace your body, the low point moves backward, and you chunk or thin it. The pause drill gives you a physical anchor you can take to the course. On hole 14, when you feel things slipping, you can literally add a micro-pause at the top and reclaim your tempo. With two sessions per week on the range, this will become second nature fast.
